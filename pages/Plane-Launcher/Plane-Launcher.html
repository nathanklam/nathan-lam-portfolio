Plane Launcher

Background

FIRST Tech Challenge

The FIRST Tech Challenge is an annual robotics competition held by FIRST (For the Inspiration and Recognition of Science and Technology). Each year, there is a new theme and field design for teams to design robots and create programs for. The competitors range from 7th to 12th grade.

Read more about it here!

For my final year in this team, the 2023-2024 season, one of the many ways to score points is to launch a paper airplane, so I dedicated much of my time in the second half of the season designing and producing the launching mechanism.

Team IFC #15157 and My Roles

Team IFC #15157 (Invicta Ferro Canibus, Latin for Invicible Iron Dogs) was founded in 2018. I have joined in the 2020-2021 season and mainly served as the Engineering Notebook editor due to the contraints imposed by the Covid-19 lockdown. Since the 2021-2022 season, I began to serve as one of the lead fabricators of the robot. In that role, I planned, designed, and built the robot and its mechanisms alongside my teammates. Starting in the 2022-2023 season, I also began to specialize in CAD, allowing me to plan the robot with the help of the software Fusion 360 while providing excellent resources for our documentation. In my final year, I was elected Vice President, but even without that title, I have always led some aspect of the team the entire time I was a member.

Iterations and Design Process

First iteration

Our initial design was a makeshift improvisation utilizing frame parts that we had at hand. As a result, many aspects were not optimized. They thus were prone to experiencing many errors (band tension loosening over time, the plane getting stuck during launch, the plane falling out in the case of any sudden robot movements, etc.).

Second Iteration

To improve this design, I decided to start from scratch. I was aware of many existing mechanisms that already exist (such as those found on a crossbow). However, I did not want to base my design on existing creations as that may cause me to exhibit convergent thinking rather than divergent thinking, which was needed in order to brainstorm and create something suitable for our task. Though it ended up having similarities to the mechanism of a crossbow, it is far more suited for our own objectives.

The process began by visualizing mechanisms and drawing them (I unfortunately did not take any photos). I then selected what I believed to be most applicable and efficient for launching a plane. Afterward, I spent countless hours creating the overall launcher on the CAD software Fusion 360. During this process, I had to constantly consider how to split up the launcher in order to allow for modularity and proper 3D printing and how to integrate it with our robot as a whole. After finally finishing the model, I printed out the parts and assembled it.

Final Results

As the printer struggled to produce the pieces to the exact specifications I desired, the final version comprised of parts of different filament colors, had additional improvisations, and was generally flimsy. Improvisations include the drilling of additional holes to allow for the addition of limbs for the elastic material. At the very least, the mechanisms work as intended, allowing for this first prototype to be efficiently loaded and taken apart while also ensuring precision and ease of use.

Reflection

Overall, the plane launcher (despite precision errors caused by the 3D printer) works as intended. It was consistent in launching the planes and easy to work with. However, I discovered that over the many hours I spent developing the launcher, the robot was also heavily iterated upon by my teammates; far more than I had expected. As a result, the launcher was not utilized on the robot as the only place it would fit would result in the robot being taller than we desired. The launcher was suitable for the robot weeks prior, not the robot of the current time. What I have learned as a result of this venture is that focusing on a smaller part is certainly an important part of engineering, but it is also important to step back occasionally in order to observe the overall system to ensure that my time spent on my part would integrate well with the final product.
